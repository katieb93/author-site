The trailer park was actually pretty fucking cool if you weren't a total dick about it. You had to know where to look, from just the right angle, and to stare directly into the incandescent string lights that only went out when storms hit our tin roof. Those nights turned my mother and me into windchimes, dancing in step to the music of the rain thud, thud, thudding, before we crashed together into the big bed. Her bed, ’cause she’d’ve slept under the axle if it meant I was warm. If it meant I grinned bigger through the thunder than anyone else in Songs River—our RV community of ne'er-do-wells who’d dipped from the system and wouldn’t shut up about it.

There, we had no government gifts like houses and sedans, doctors and vacations. No perks and protection like everyone who lived by the rules. Instead, we had each other and electricity from a plug at the back of our van. My mom would pull me close and whisper fantasies while the oak outside thrashed in the wind, hammering the bedroom window. She’d call me “beautiful, beautiful girl,” and tell me about growing wings and flying away to see the windmills of the Netherlands. Stuff she’d read in books.

I’d whisper back never-will-bes, future things that sounded like cool ideas—lights that made green things grow inside the house, gardens that bloomed even in winter. A phone small enough to fit in my hand, one that could hold every song I loved. Ideas, stories—just stories.

It was 2013, and in a few weeks, I’d be the same age as the new millennium. Thirteen, and all my mom’s nicknames for me were budding and dying before I could stop loving them. “Beautiful, beautiful girl” quickly died the moment my tits entered the scene, right before we stopped sharing a pillow.

Then I became *Smarty Pants McGee* after I kept winning awards for my propagation skills and started bringing home prizes like watering cans and rakes. The day I got home with a brass shovel and tampons for the very first time, my mom’s barely-there congratulations had me thinking Trix was all she’d ever call me again. That all my McGees had been plundered in the days of counting the seconds after lightning.

My very last one arrived just as autumn blew in. October in Texas had us on our knees for rain, so I was still doing all the watering myself instead of the sky. When I stepped into our van, mud was thick beneath my nails.

I hadn’t been the only one outside. Never was when we could wear sweaters without sweating. I’d said hi to everyone while wearing long sleeves and shorts. My mother was getting ready for work, the hair dryer blowing from the bathroom.

“You’ll never guess what someone called me just now!” I shouted out, turning the kitchen sink on, trying to overcome all the noise.

She could hardly hear me but still called back, “Tell me!”

“Just take a guess,” I said, scraping the dirt from my palms and having a hard time ’cause it was thick and rich and meant for wildflowers, which wouldn’t arrive until spring.

I was being good. I was being patient. I was waiting for things to grow. Like they’d told me to. My teachers and the ladies who came to talk to us at school, who asked us about what we liked to do for fun and which boys we thought were cute. Women with claw-clips in their hair who told me I was pretty and to speak softly.

But I never did. And never would learn how.

“Uh… Chickadee McGee… Washington?” my mother asked, the water and the blow dryer turning off in tandem, like we’d meant to do it in step with each other.

“Hahaha, so funny,” I called back, so dry my mouth watered. My cheeks were bursting at the McGee as I opened the fridge for any kind of anything we might have lying around. “No.”

She’d promised to get the good pudding—the swirl kind—so I didn’t see her face when she came into the kitchen. I was too busy looking through cold jars and jams, sliding over her orange juice, the kind she drank after work. The bottle I kept pretending not to know was mostly, at this point, tequila.

“So what, then?” she asked. “What’d they call you?”

The swirls were behind my orange juice with just a splash of grenadine.

“Beautiful, beautiful girl.” I smiled. “Isn’t that crazy?”

My mother whipped the fridge door so far back it broke off its hinges. We didn’t have fridge door money. We barely had swirl money.

“Who the fuck said that to you?”

The question slammed me to the floor, had me scrambling for the answer even though I already knew it.

Her stare pinned me harder, yanking me back to kindergarten, preschool even, when the only cure for every hurt was clutching her knee. But now she wasn’t healing me. She was hurtling me forward, straight into a fear I never wanted to get used to.

“Indy,” I said.

Indy was just some dude aging too quickly who never meant any harm, never really meant anything. Always lived halfway out his window, shirts ripped on the thorny latch. He’d been one of the ones to say hey to me that morning. A neighbor I’d always known.

“Indy?” My mother caught a break in her voice before it could snap in half.

I nodded. “Yeah. Just Indy.”

“Stay here, Beatrix,” she said. Not Trix.

“Stay here?” My vowels shrank down ’cause she’d never told me that, not once. ’Cause there was never anything out there that could get me. No creeping hands shuffling through the windows, disembodied, ready to slither under the covers.

Damned if she wasn’t acting like there could be.

“Stay. Here.” Her hand sliced through the air as she rolled her shoulders back, readying for something I couldn’t see. Didn’t know existed.

If she’d slammed our front door any harder, we’d’ve lost two doors that day. But the tin one stayed on and jostled against itself, trying to decide whether to slip off or hold tight.
