Beatrix "Trix" Sarett comes of age in Songs River, a trailer park on the edge of Faubion, Texas, where storms slap the tin roof and neighbors drink cheap beer under string lights. Her mother, Sasha, is both shield and storm, loving too hard and drinking too much. She hides Trix's preternatural beauty because in this America, reshaped by a deadlier 1918 flu and a century of population collapse, the Hearth Program controls family life. Every household is enrolled. Every girl receives a pamphlet the way boys receive draft letters. When Trix turns thirteen, hers arrives. The demand is clear: sign here, commit to three children in five years, and repopulate the nation. Turns out Sasha isn't paranoid.

By the time she's fifteen, home is unbearable for Trix. Sasha threatens to disfigure her face rather than let the Program claim her. Trix runs away for good and ends up sleeping in the community garden, where she steals the plants in order to eat. Terrified of any form of control, Trix manages to evade all adults, foster care (or worse) on the horizon. She is finally tracked down by Francesca, her placement evaluator. Acting as friend, social worker, and match-maker, Francesca begins to hover. Faced with the choice of sending her back to Sasha or surrendering her to early placement, Francesca arranges for Trix to stay with a family friend until her sixteenth birthday, when the Program will claim her for good.

By nineteen, Trix has two children: Rose, three, a firecracker, and Iris, two, who clings to her side. Her assigned husband, Gabriel, twenty-six, is kind, sincere, and their home hums with fragile ease. Trix gardens, Gabriel works, and at home, they flirt in the kitchen. But the Hearth Program does not forget. It has been four years since her placement, and she has borne only two children. The third must come within the next year. If she still does not conceive, officials will declare the household "unstable." In the name of Family Stability, she will be removed and Gabriel reassigned a new wife while Rose and Iris remain with him. Her love for them keeps her tethered even as the mandate hollows her body into nothing but a womb on deadline.

Terrified and looking for sympathy, Trix turns to her friend Thelma, a barista who admits to being part of a secret network helping women escape the nation. She urges Trix to leave that night. But Trix hesitates, fearing her daughters would face the same poverty and homelessness she did. Instead, she devotes herself to compliance: twice-daily sex, fertility tracking, every period a greater threat to her family. But with the idea of escape planted, the illusion of her gentle, lovely life begins to fracture. Thelma won't let the idea of escape rest, however, and deals Trix the final blow. She slips a confidential report into Trix's mailbox: she and Gabriel were never paired by the system. Gabriel picked her himself. After his graduation, he found her file in his father's study, where his 60-year-old uncle, newly widowed, was considering her as a potential bride. Gabriel seized her page to save her but buried the truth beneath years of silence. For Trix, the deepest betrayal is not that he picked her, but that he lied about it for their entire marriage. With her husband unapologetic and the Program's clock at its end, Trix finally seizes Thelma's offer of escape. Heading west, they flee with Rose and Iris.

In flashbacks, the day after Trix's sweet sixteen, Francesca flies her to Austin, where she is married to Gabriel. He's twenty-two, freshly graduated, and buys her a birthday cake after they say their vows. At first, she bristles against every new experience. She is still a teenager, still a sophomore in high school, and now a wife. But placement brings a beautiful house, a tree-covered neighborhood, a partner whose steadiness is real shelter. In spite of herself, she softens to Gabriel, keeps going to school, and even joins the spring play. When she learns she is pregnant, her terror dissipates into her natural resilience. By the time Rose arrives, she commands the room and gives birth alone, making Gabriel wait outside. She wants independence, air, and autonomy.

At twenty-years-old and on the run with Rose and Iris at twenty-years-old, the road strips away every protection for Trix. At first, it feels like relief: the girls asleep in Thelma's borrowed car as the city fades. But they cannot drive far without raising suspicion. They move on to a rural church northwest of Abilene, hidden in a Sunday school room for one night. For a moment, Trix almost lets herself believe they are safe. But a laugh slips through the door, and in the dead of night, the Reverend tells them to flee. With no car, Thelma runs for help while Trix hides with the girls in the brush. Rose grows clammy, and by the time Thelma returns with a borrowed car, the fever rages.

Their next stop is a sham motel run by the Nelsons, part of a resistance called the Kindling. Mrs. Nelson offers shelter. Her daughter Georgia, a nurse, agrees to drive them to the border, even as Iris begins to sicken just as Rose begins to get better. Georgia agrees to drive them to the border. That night, Thelma cuts through a rancher's fence. Georgia lingers. A man with a rifle steps from the dark. Thelma turns back to help her. Trix runs, Iris on her hip, Rose's hand in hers. Behind them, a single shot. Thelma doesn't follow.

They take shelter in a RV in the middle of White Sands, New Mexico. Trix's belly begins to betray her when she realizes she is pregnant. The desert presses in. Every morning, she locks her daughters inside the RV and walks into the dunes only to return empty-handed. Trix resolves that if her daughters die, it will not be while waiting in an abandoned shell of a home. It will be in her arms and moving forward.
Trix, girls in her arms, stumbles through the desert until the silence is interrupted by a hallucination of Sasha. Heat and thirst blur memory and present; Sasha calls her old nicknames until Trix cannot tell if she is twelve again or already a mother. Starving, Trix faints. When she looks up again, Iris and Rose are gone, and she no longer knows if she has saved anyone at all.

Then, Trix realizes she's being rescued. Francesca, unseen since her wedding day, carries her and the girls to a health clinic. As Iris recovers, Francesca reveals she now leads the resistance. Trix's case changed her. She had been flagged too young, placed too early, pregnant at sixteen twice. Francesca saw she wasn't being protected but used. She couldn't forget her. So, Francesca would not let her sink into contentment, not when saving her might redeem her.She recruited Thelma and slipped birth control into Trix's coffee. Not to protect her, but to provoke the system. If Trix failed her quota, the Program would tighten its grip. Francesca believed the pressure would make her run. And it did.

For Trix, this is the final deceit. Her body was used again, her life shaped by choices made without her consent. She realizes no one has ever allowed her to decide. Not Sasha, not Gabriel, not Francesca, not even Thelma. Each of them believed they were saving her. None of them trusted her to save herself. Fleeing anyone else's idea of refuge, they drive toward an unknown border, not sure what lies ahead, but certain no one else will decide for them again.
