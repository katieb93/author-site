"Listen, I bear Anya no ill will, and she’s still, I mean, she’s still a teenager. People forget that. We were pretty close for a few months, and she was coming off a really scary relationship with a much, much older guy, and I think that messed with her head for a while. I’m not going to go into details; that’s her story to tell if she wants to tell it. For my part, I tried to be a good friend in a rough time in her life, but it's tricky, you know, the demands of someone who needs so much more than you can possibly give them.”

— Charlie Devereaux on the podcast Things I Think You Should Know

__NOW | ONE__

When I first met Anya Ford, she was smoking a cigarette on the curb of Elm Street, and the tattoo of her face was still healing on my wrist.

It took her a second to notice me. It took me a couple thousand more to accept that she was real—walked straight out of my television and onto the gravel roads of my hometown. She held my gaze as if we knew each other as if I wasn’t a stranger. Of course, I was to her. 

She was not a stranger to me. I’d grown up beside her image since sixth grade.

Then, she looked past me, distracted—Charlie’s quote hung in the air. Only Anya could hear it, and for a precious few hours, she could hold onto it, keep it a secret. The words obscured me, obscured everything, even the streetlamp, even the light. To spite them, she spoke, and her ‘hey’ was blithe.

So, so much of her hair was not her own. It hung in clumps, lame, to her shoulders, which were far thinner than I thought a person’s could be. As if she’d just been rescued from a windy mountaintop, a tap on her jacket might’ve tipped her right over. 

She had no reason to be here. No one who wasn’t born into Faubion, Texas, had a reason to be here. But Anya Ford? Anya Ford?

I had to stop using her last name. I would, eventually.

I always thought I’d handle myself better—more poised, prepared—for something so far-fetched. I’d imagined meeting her behind the gates of a red carpet, standing in the shadow of a dazzle. Instead, she was sitting on cracked pavement in the dim glow of the last working streetlamp within five miles.

And all I could say was, “Oh my God.”

She forgave me for it quickly, but to this day, I wish I’d said something she actually needed to hear.

I’ve always wanted to be psychic.

Don had sent me home from the diner with a fancy IPA because I’d stayed halfway through the next shift. Anya glanced at it, asked if I was going to drink it, and when I handed it over, her arm slipped out of the light and into the darkness where I stood. 

“I’ll buy you another, she said.”

I didn’t tell her that no store within an hour of us sold them. Don brought them home from the bougie grocery store when he visited his kids in Austin. I wouldn’t get the chance to have another for several months.

Anya drank it like water, as if it cured thirst, and thanked me, the thought having occurred to her only after she got what she wanted.

On the curb, knees to her chest, she sat quiet, like I should be the one to keep the conversation going, which was wild because all I could think about at the time was the drifting sleeve of my sweater and how it inched up my arm.

I couldn’t have known that “much, much older” was louder than me. That “messed with her head” was ACL, Lollapalooza. But then again, I was whispering, afraid to spook my dream away. 
“What are you doing here?”

It was all I could think to ask because, “what is it like to be you?” was stupid, and :God, I want to be you,” was even worse.
Anya shrugged. “I was going to the diner but needed cell reception, so I kept walking.” The crickets filled the silence, stretching it wide. She pulled out another cigarette and lit it. “Then I couldn’t find my way back. So I found the brightest street and sat down.”

“But why?”

She exhaled, “Someone always finds me.”

“Do you have AT&T?”

“Yeah.”

“AT&T doesn’t work here. That’s probably why your phone’s out.”

“Oh.”

Anya would have to be taught how to ask questions about others, a child learning the difference between their eyes and ears.
“The diner’s down this street, then to the right. The last turn might’ve thrown you off,” I hesitated, then, “That was your car? The cute one?”

“The Prius? Yeah. I rented it.”

I’d seen the car drive in, headlamp’s light falling through the water-stained windows. But I walked to the back, waiting for Arlo to clock in. My hands had been caked with soda, and the grime still lingered in the cracks of my palms. I hadn’t thought anything of it until the idea of shaking Anya’s hand became a possibility. I didn’t think much of the car either, except that it was nicer than we usually saw in Faubion, nice but covered in dust, which meant the driver had come from far away. Yonder, even.

“I can walk you back.”

Anya didn’t move. “That’s okay.”

“I can sit with you.”

This time, she flinched. Just slightly. Just enough.

I stepped back. “You’re in a bad area.”

She dropped her cigarette to the curb and stomped it out with her Doc Marten. We wore the same ones, the exact same ones, but her’s were smaller. Everything about her was small, save the space she took up in my mind. 

She lit another one immediately and I resisted telling her that we wore the same shoes.

“I stopped wanting to walk.”

It was not helpful, she was being purposefully caustic. She wanted to be left alone to turn to dust. To be kidnapped. To be found by someone who would take her picture and post it and let the whole world know its sweetheart was finally having the breakdown we always thought she would have. The world would think that in a few hours anyway.
Charlie, Charlie, Charlie. Charlie spoke so well. 

I laughed because I couldn’t believe what was happening. My heroine petulant and drained of life at my feet. 

“I can’t leave you here alone,” I said finally. “Even if you weren’t... you. I’m not joking when I say you could, like, literally die.”

She looked up at me again, and I always kind of thought she would appear more like a human in person. Instead, she looked just the same as she always did.

“Do you need a place to sleep?”

She’d been waiting for some displeasure. Craved it like I craved legitimacy. 

“That motel looked pretty fucked.”

“No! Sorry, no, don’t ever go there. You, especially, shouldn’t go there.”

Her funny little grin was real.

“I mean, if you need it, we have a spare room.”

Anya looked at me for too long, and I thought she thought I was out of my mind for offering such a thing. Had I been her, I would have cried at the idea, at the chance for some relief. She was delicate and exacting in how she moved, lighter than air in how she let her hand fall to the side, close to her body. 

“Yeah.” She said, her nod a concert just for me, “Okay.”

She stood up as if the wind moved her. We never shook hands, her grief an aura all its own. No one could breach it, and I had no idea what to say. I could not speak, what with fate running up and knocking me down. My breath enveloped me as I moved through it, and I paused to try and deal with what was right in front of my eyes. 

When I hung back just a second too long, Anya turned to me, taking the cigarette out of her mouth to speak. Then she finally saw me and realized I did not have what completed a person.

“You didn’t tell me your name.” 

“Evelyn,” I told her. “Evelyn Hyde.” 

“I like your tattoo, Evelyn,” Anya said with mercy as if she could have known what saying such a thing would mean to me. “Most I’ve seen aren’t so classy.”
